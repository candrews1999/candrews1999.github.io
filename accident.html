Hawaii Missile Scare

In January 2018, a wireless emergency alert (WEA) mistakenly informed the state of Hawaii of a non-existent ballistic missile threat. The event sent the public into panic for 38 minutes, at which time the Hawaii Emergency Managment Agency (HEMA) informed the public that it was a false alarm. I investigated the major flaws in the protocol and software that led to the accident in an effort to design solutions that would improve HEMA's ability to keep the public well informed.

Analysis

Who's to Blame?

Since the introduction of WEAs in 2012, this black swan occurrence exemplifies of the need to safeguard against false alarms within this public messaging platform. The FCC claims the accident resulted from a lack of "reasonable safeguards and process controls", but how did the state's alibi differ from the HEMA operator's (Employee 1) alibi?

Investigating the WEA Software

In order to better understand the software in use by HEMA, I investigated the process which operators follow to send a WEA. AlertSense is a public messaging software similar to the system utilized in the accident.

HEMA's System of Sending WEAs

Mapping out the system of sending WEAs allowed me to better understand the roles of the software, operator, and HEMA and how they collectively produced the accident.
